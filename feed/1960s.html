Introduction to 1960s India

The Changing Face of India

The 1960s marked a decade of significant transition for India. Just over a decade into independence, the nation was balancing its newfound identity with growing global connections. This period witnessed the strengthening of democratic institutions alongside major challenges from wars with China (1962) and Pakistan (1965), as well as internal political shifts including the rise of Indira Gandhi to power.

As the world experienced cultural and social revolutions, India too was witnessing gradual transformations in its urban centers. The decade represented a fascinating blend of traditional values and emerging modernity, with cinema, music, and fashion reflecting these changing times. The Green Revolution began transforming agricultural practices, while new industries continued developing under the mixed economy model.

History Politics Cultural Change

Political Landscape

The political landscape of India in the 1960s underwent significant transformations. The decade began with Jawaharlal Nehru still at the helm, but his death in 1964 marked the end of an era. Lal Bahadur Shastri became Prime Minister but his tenure was brief, ending with his untimely death in 1966. This paved the way for Indira Gandhi to become India's first female Prime Minister.

This period saw the Congress party's dominance begin to face challenges, with the emergence of regional parties and growing opposition. The decade ended with the Congress party splitting in 1969 into Congress (O) and Congress (R), with the latter led by Indira Gandhi, signaling the beginning of a new political chapter in India's history.

Key Features of 1960s India

Wars & Defense

India fought wars with China (1962) and Pakistan (1965), leading to significant changes in defense policies and international relations.

Green Revolution

Agricultural reforms introduced high-yielding seeds, modern farming techniques, and irrigation improvements, moving India toward food self-sufficiency.

Space Program

INCOSPAR was established in 1962, launching India's first sounding rocket in 1963 and evolving into ISRO by the end of the decade.

Music & Cinema

The Golden Age of Film Music

The 1960s are considered the golden age of Hindi film music, with legendary composers like S.D. Burman, Shankar-Jaikishan, and R.D. Burman creating timeless melodies. Mohammad Rafi, Lata Mangeshkar, and Kishore Kumar dominated playback singing.

Songs from films like "Mughal-e-Azam" (1960), "Guide" (1965), and "Aradhana" (1969) continue to enchant listeners decades later, representing the perfect blend of classical Indian music with emerging global influences.

Bollywood Film Music R.D. Burman

Cinema's New Wave

While commercial cinema continued to thrive with stars like Raj Kapoor, Dilip Kumar, and Nargis, the 1960s also witnessed the emergence of parallel cinema led by directors like Satyajit Ray, Ritwik Ghatak, and Mrinal Sen.

Films began exploring more complex social themes and artistic expressions, with Ray's "Charulata" (1964) and "Nayak" (1966) receiving international acclaim and establishing Indian cinema's presence on the global stage.

Satyajit Ray Parallel Cinema Film Festival

The Radio Evolution

All India Radio continued its expansion, introducing more diverse programming including the immensely popular "Binaca Geetmala" countdown show hosted by Ameen Sayani, which became a national institution.

Radio remained the primary source of entertainment and information for most Indians, particularly in rural areas. The introduction of Vividh Bharati service in 1957 had expanded through the 1960s, offering light entertainment programming.

All India Radio Vividh Bharati Binaca Geetmala

Iconic Playback Singers

The 1960s solidified the careers of legendary playback singers who became the voices of Indian cinema. Mohammad Rafi was at his peak, while Kishore Kumar transitioned from acting to full-time singing by the end of the decade.

Female playback was dominated by the incomparable Lata Mangeshkar and her sister Asha Bhosle, who between them sang most of the female songs in Hindi films. Their distinctive vocal styles defined the sound of 1960s Indian music.

Mohammad Rafi Lata Mangeshkar Kishore Kumar

Major Events

Indo-China War (1962)

The brief but impactful border conflict with China came as a shock to India's leadership and citizens alike. The war ended with a unilateral Chinese ceasefire after China claimed victory, leaving India to reassess its defense policies and military preparedness.

This conflict fundamentally altered India's foreign policy outlook and accelerated defense modernization efforts. It also led to closer ties with the Soviet Union in subsequent years as Cold War alignments became more pronounced.

War Foreign Policy Defense
October-November 1962

Indo-Pakistan War (1965)

The second major conflict between India and Pakistan since independence began with Operation Gibraltar, Pakistan's infiltration attempt in Kashmir. The war expanded to multiple fronts before a ceasefire was arranged through Soviet mediation.

The subsequent Tashkent Agreement, signed by Indian Prime Minister Lal Bahadur Shastri and Pakistani President Ayub Khan, restored pre-war positions. Tragically, Shastri died in Tashkent shortly after signing the agreement.

War Kashmir Tashkent Agreement
August-September 1965

Green Revolution Begins

Under the leadership of M.S. Swaminathan and with support from Norman Borlaug, India initiated agricultural reforms that introduced high-yielding variety seeds, modern farming techniques, irrigation improvements, and fertilizers.

These changes dramatically increased food grain production, particularly wheat and rice, helping India move towards food self-sufficiency by the early 1970s and averting the famine that many international experts had predicted.

Agriculture Food Security Rural Development
1965-1967

Fashion & Lifestyle

Women's Fashion

The 1960s saw Indian women's fashion begin to incorporate more global influences. While sarees remained dominant, their styling evolved with more experimental blouse designs and bold patterns. The salwar kameez gained further popularity among young women.

Bollywood actresses like Sadhana popularized the "Sadhana cut" (fringe hairstyle), while Sharmila Tagore's bouffants became fashion statements. In metropolitan areas, some young women began experimenting with Western clothes including A-line dresses and high boots.

Sadhana Cut Bouffant Saree Styles

Men's Fashion

Indian men's fashion in the 1960s showed stronger Western influences. The slim-fit "Beatles suit" gained popularity among urban youth, while bell-bottoms began appearing toward the end of the decade.

The formal Nehru jacket remained popular for official occasions. Film stars like Dev Anand and Shammi Kapoor set trends with their distinctive styles, influencing young men across the country.

Beatles Suit Bell Bottoms Dev Anand

Urban Lifestyle

The 1960s saw increasing urbanization and the emergence of a more visible middle class in cities. Coffee houses and restaurants became social hubs where intellectuals, artists and students gathered for discussions on politics, arts and social change.

The Ambassador car remained a status symbol for the middle class, while scooters offered mobility to many families. Modern appliances like refrigerators and transistor radios began appearing in more urban households.

Coffee Houses Ambassador Car Appliances

Ideologies & Movements

Growing Socialist Influence

The 1960s saw India's economic policies move toward greater state control and socialist principles. Under Nehru and later Indira Gandhi, major industries were nationalized, with the banking sector's nationalization in 1969 marking a significant shift toward socialist economic policies.

These policies were influenced partly by India's growing ties with the Soviet Union, which became an important ally after the 1962 Indo-China war. The Cold War context shaped many of India's policy decisions during this period.

Socialism Nationalization Soviet Influence

Student Movements

University campuses across India became centers of political activism, mirroring global student movements. The All India Students Federation (AISF) and other student organizations gained prominence.

While not as widespread as in Western countries, Indian students began questioning authority and traditional values, particularly in urban centers like Delhi, Mumbai, and Kolkata.

Student Politics Youth Movement Universities

Language Movements

The 1960s witnessed significant language-based political movements, particularly in southern India. The Anti-Hindi agitations in Tamil Nadu (then Madras State) in 1965 were a powerful response to the planned implementation of Hindi as the sole official language.

These protests led to the continuation of the two-language formula and acknowledgment of regional linguistic identities, significantly influencing India's language policy for decades to come.

Anti-Hindi Movement Tamil Nadu Language Policy

Naxalite Movement

The Naxalite movement emerged in 1967 with a peasant uprising in Naxalbari, West Bengal. Inspired by Maoist ideology, it represented a radical leftist approach to addressing rural inequality and land ownership issues.

The movement attracted many intellectuals and students, particularly in West Bengal and parts of Andhra Pradesh, marking the beginning of what would become a long-term conflict between far-left groups and the Indian state.

Naxalbari Maoism Peasant Uprising

Technology & Science

Space Research Begins

The Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR) was established in 1962 under the Department of Atomic Energy, with Dr. Vikram Sarabhai as its chairman. This later evolved into ISRO in 1969.

The first sounding rocket was launched from Thumba Equatorial Rocket Launching Station in Kerala in 1963, marking India's entry into space research despite being a developing nation with limited resources.

ISRO Vikram Sarabhai Space Research

Nuclear Development

India's nuclear program continued developing under the leadership of Dr. Homi Bhabha until his untimely death in 1966. The Atomic Energy Establishment at Trombay (renamed Bhabha Atomic Research Centre in 1967) expanded its activities.

The Tarapur Atomic Power Station, India's first nuclear power plant, began construction in 1962 and was commissioned in 1969, marking a significant step in India's nuclear energy program.

Nuclear Energy Homi Bhabha BARC

Agricultural Technology

The Green Revolution introduced mechanization including tractors and modern irrigation systems to Indian agriculture, though initially limited to certain regions, particularly Punjab, Haryana, and western Uttar Pradesh.

Scientific research in developing high-yielding varieties of wheat and rice was conducted through collaborations between Indian scientists and international agricultural research institutes.

Green Revolution Mechanization High-Yield Crops

Industrial Technology

The 1960s saw India's industrial base continuing to expand under the Five-Year Plans. Heavy industries established in the previous decade were now producing machine tools, electrical equipment, and other industrial products.

Efforts to develop indigenous technology were promoted through institutions like the Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) and the establishment of Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs) which began producing skilled engineers for the growing industrial sector.

Manufacturing Five Year Plan IIT

Daily Life

College Life in the 1960s

My grandmother's stories of her college days in Delhi University during the mid-1960s paint a fascinating picture of youth culture in urban India. "Our campus was becoming increasingly political," she recalls. "The world was changing, and we felt we could be part of that change."

She describes how Western music – particularly The Beatles and Bob Dylan – began influencing young Indians. "We would gather in someone's home who owned a record player, and listen to these new sounds while discussing everything from existentialism to socialism."

Yet traditions remained strong. "Dating was still taboo," she laughs. "We had 'friendships' instead. And most of us still lived with our families, following their rules." This balance of embracing new ideas while maintaining cultural continuity defined the experience of many urban youth during this transformative decade.

The 1965 War Through a Child's Eyes

My father was just 9 years old during the 1965 Indo-Pak war, growing up in a small town near Amritsar. He remembers the blackouts most vividly - how every evening all lights had to be covered or turned off so as not to provide targets for enemy aircraft.

"It was both scary and exciting for a child," he told me. "We would huddle together by candlelight, listening to radio updates. The adults spoke in hushed tones, but we children created our own adventures, pretending to be soldiers defending the border."

He recalls how the community came together, with neighbors checking on each other and sharing resources. "Even though there was fear, there was also remarkable unity. People who normally didn't speak to each other because of different religions or backgrounds suddenly became one community."

Memories of 1960s Cinema

My father often recalls how the landscape of Indian cinema transformed during the 1960s. "It wasn't just about escapist entertainment anymore," he would tell me. "Films started reflecting our society's complexities and contradictions."

He vividly remembers watching Satyajit Ray's "Mahanagar" (The Big City) in 1964, which portrayed a woman entering the workforce against social expectations. "The theater was unusually quiet after the film ended," he recalls. "People were actually discussing the themes rather than just humming the songs. Something was changing in how we engaged with cinema."

Equally impactful was Guru Dutt's "Sahib Bibi Aur Ghulam" from the early 60s. "That film showed the decay of the feudal system through such powerful imagery," he would say, "and Meena Kumari's performance as Chhoti Bahu was haunting." These films, alongside the more commercial offerings, marked a significant evolution in Indian storytelling.

Vikram Mehta

The Green Revolution's Impact

My grandfather was a farmer in Punjab when the Green Revolution transformed agriculture in the late 1960s. He often described the dramatic change from traditional farming methods to the new techniques.

"The government agricultural extension officers would visit our village regularly," he recounted. "They brought these new seeds - we called them miracle seeds - and taught us about chemical fertilizers and pesticides. Many farmers were skeptical at first, including me."

But the results were undeniable. "Our wheat yield nearly tripled within two years. Suddenly, we had surplus to sell. By 1970, I had enough money to buy my first tractor, something I never imagined owning." The prosperity brought new opportunities - my father became the first in our family to attend college, changing our family's trajectory forever.

Ranveer Gill

Life in Newly Built Chandigarh

My grandmother's family moved to Chandigarh in the early 1960s when the city was still being built. She has fascinating stories about living in Le Corbusier's modernist vision while it was taking shape.

"It was like living in two different worlds," she says. "The city sectors were planned with such precision – wide roads, modern buildings, and plenty of green spaces. But just outside the planned city, traditional Indian life continued unchanged for centuries."

She recalls the pride people felt about the city. "We were part of something new and progressive. The geometric Capitol Complex, with its massive concrete structures, seemed futuristic to us. Everyone who visited was amazed at how different it was from other Indian cities. It represented our hopes for modern India."

Priya Sharma
September 14, 2025
